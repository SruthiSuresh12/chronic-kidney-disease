Introduction

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is a long-term condition marked by the gradual loss of kidney function over time. The kidneys are vital organs responsible for filtering waste, balancing fluids, and regulating blood pressure. When they become damaged, waste products and excess fluid can build up in the body, leading to serious health complications. CKD often progresses silently, showing few symptoms until advanced stages, making early detection and management essential for preventing irreversible damage.

What Causes CKD?

  • Comorbidities

    Diabetes and high blood pressure, which damage kidney blood vessels over time

  • Infections

    Chronic kidney infections, autoimmune diseases, and inherited disorders such as polycystic kidney disease

  • Medication

    Long-term urinary tract obstructions or repeated use of medications toxic to the kidneys

Symptoms of CKD

Early stages of CKD are typically asymptomatic. As kidney function declines, symptoms may include fatigue, swelling in the legs and ankles, changes in urination, shortness of breath, and confusion. Diagnosis relies on blood tests (measuring creatinine and estimating glomerular filtration rate), urine tests (for protein or blood), and imaging studies. Regular screening is recommended for at-risk populations to enable timely intervention.
